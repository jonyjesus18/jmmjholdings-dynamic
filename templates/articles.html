NVIDIA, an ESG darling

November 10th, 2021 by Joao

ESG Environment Technology

With the advent of Machine learning and artificial intelligence, the machines NVIDIA has been building for years, have now found even greater importance in the tech world. NVIDIA's graphic cards allow for much more efficient computing and better performance. However, as Nvidia scaled up, so did the need for more silicon. A larger pressure on the sourcing of silicon and rare minerals for chip-making means Nvidias has taken place as one of the largest ESG companies in the world. However, apart from phenomenal technological achievements and stock gains, Nvidia does not lag in the fields of Material sourcing and energy use.

Technology backed improvement

As an example, Nvidia's end-to-end AI supercomputing platform powered by NVIDIA Ampere architecture A100 GPUs are up to 40x more energy efficient than traditional CPU servers when running AI and high-performance computing workloads. According to NVIDIAs 2021 ESG report, If all CPU servers running AI and HPC transitioned to GPUs, the world would save 11 terawatt-hours of electricity per year—equivalent to taking two coal-fired power plants offline or removing 1.7M cars from the road.

NVIDIA is also a member of the Public Private Alliance (PPA) for Responsible Minerals Trade, and the Responsible Minerals Initiative (RMI). The PPA provides funding and coordination support to organizations working within the Democratic Republic of Congo and adjacent countries to develop verifiable conflict-free supply chains.

On top of this, Nvidia's sustainability report points out its gross margin, or per cent of profit left from revenue after paying direct costs actually rose to 62.3% in fiscal 2021. That's up from 61.2% in fiscal 2019. Additionally, revenue jumped more than 40% in that time.

Showing it's far from resting, Nvidia outlined 24 ESG investing goals for 2022, all of which that can be checked here. Nvidia is just another great example of how technology can be employed to save on emissions and improve the welfare of our planet. Soon, many companies will follow Nvidia's lead.

The role of Lithium in the new economy

November 5th, 2021 by Joao

ESG Environment Technology

Although we use it every day, on our laptops, cars or phones, Lithium production has important sustainability challenges that need to be addressed to fulfil its role in the transition into a low carbon economy. Despite lithium’s role, the controversial nature of the sustainability of lithium operations is rising

Some things come at a cost

The majority of lithium is currently extracted from only 10 sites around the world found in Australia, Argentina, Chile, and China. This geographical disparity is a challenge for the rest of the world, especially for regions like the EU with a big ambition to go net-zero. Other challenges can be political and financial issues that affect the profitability of lithium projects. For example, while Bolivia has the largest lithium resources of the world, it does not have a strong commercial extraction of the product yet. As any raw material, there are important social and environmental challenges that arise from the extraction process.

In Portugal, local authorities have received immense backlash after allowing for lithium extraction near the border with Spain. Lithium extraction is resource-intensive, therefore, it represents water stress, land use, and pollution risks. Social issues must be addressed with tools like community consultation, engagement, and free, prior, informed consent. A thorough environmental and social impact assessment and the establishment of maximum extraction levels can aid in the reduction of environmental risks.

Recycling electric batteries is a developing sector that could help fulfil the world’s need for lithium and reduce its environmental and social impacts. Higher pressure in the raw materials market will increment the need and profitability of recycling lithium accompanied by environmental policy to steer the sector into end-of-life practices. The Li-ion battery recycling market is projected to grow from USD 1.5 billion in 2019 to USD 18.1 billion by 2030. Other alternatives are being studied to alleviate the environmental impact of the extraction process, for example, lithium extraction from geothermal waters where geothermal energy is naturally powering the lithium extraction from the solid rock.

Water ETFs

October 5th, 2021 by Joao

ESG Environment

A short review on often-forgotten ETF's

With wildfires being caused due to water shortages and rising temperatures, and crops in California risking obliteration from continuous drought, it seems brings back to the table the old idea that we need to value what we intrinsically need the most - water. Some water ETF's this year have attained very high valuations. Is it just the market rise or is there intrinsic value in the product? President Biden’s infrastructure bill includes $55 billion for water infrastructure and other water system related investments. Investing in water could be a good long-term bet for those concerned about its sustainability. It is also a good option for environmentally-focused investors.

The First Trust Water ETF (FIW) invests in companies that derive a large portion of their revenues from the potable and wastewater industry. With a 1 Year average 40% return, the ETF is beating competitors, which rather focus more on companies that create products designed to conserve and purify water. Invesco Water Resources ETF is perhaps the clear competitor. The market is indicating the strong sentiment in the water space, but is unclear, however, which niche segments are the ones with the strongest ability to keep sustainable growth. An interesting area for further research, especially around consumer sentiment and the different segments of the water supply chain.
